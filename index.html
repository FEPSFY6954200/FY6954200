Abstract

Quantum computing represents a significant departure from classical computation by exploiting quantum mechanical principles such as superposition and entanglement. This paper outlines the fundamental concepts of quantum computing and examines how these principles enable certain quantum algorithms to outperform classical approaches for specific problems. Key algorithms and applications are discussed, alongside the practical challenges faced by current Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) devices, including decoherence, error rates, and limited scalability. While quantum computing shows strong theoretical promise, its current impact remains constrained by hardware limitations. The paper concludes by considering future developments, emphasising the role of advances in error correction, hardware design, and hybrid quantum–classical approaches.

Introduction

Although classical computers have advanced significantly, they remain limited when addressing highly complex tasks such as molecular simulation and high-dimensional optimisation. These limitations arise partly because classical bits exist only in binary states, restricting the amount of information that can be processed simultaneously. In contrast, quantum computing utilises qubits that exploit quantum mechanical principles such as superposition and entanglement, enabling information to be represented and manipulated in fundamentally richer ways. Current quantum systems operate within the Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) era, where functional quantum devices exist but remain highly susceptible to noise and hardware imperfections. While NISQ devices are not yet capable of solving large-scale real-world problems, they provide a valuable platform for testing early quantum algorithms and exploring emerging approaches such as quantum machine learning. This paper examines the core principles of quantum computing, reviews key quantum algorithms, and discusses both the remaining challenges and potential future applications of the field. However, despite strong theoretical promise, current hardware limitations mean that quantum computing’s practical performance gains remain constrained.

Analysis and discussion

Principles of Quantum Computing:

2.1 Superposition

Superposition is one of the central ideas in quantum computing. Unlike classical bits, qubits can exist in coherent superpositions of states, enabling parallel state evaluation within quantum algorithms, which is written as:

∣ψ⟩=α∣0⟩+β∣1⟩,

where the amplitudes determine the measurement probabilities (Nielsen & Chuang). This ability to exist in multiple states at once is a major reason why quantum computers might eventually outperform classical ones. See fig 1 for a representation of a single qubit

2.2 Entanglement

Entanglement is another key concept in quantum mechanics. When two qubits become entangled, the state of one qubit depends on the state of the other, even when they are far apart (Nielsen & Chuang). Entanglement is essential for many quantum algorithms and is also widely used in quantum machine learning approaches (Biamonte et al., 2017).This distinction highlights why quantum systems are fundamentally different from classical computers; however, exploiting these properties reliably remains a major experimental challenge

2.3 Quantum Logic Gates

Quantum gates manipulate qubits in specific ways. The Pauli X, Y, and Z gates, as well as the Hadamard gate, are used to rotate or flip a qubit’s state. The CNOT gate creates entanglement between qubits and is a fundamental building block for quantum circuits (Nielsen & Chuang). These gates allow quantum algorithms to be built similarly to how classical logic gates form classical circuits, but with quantum mechanical behaviour.

3. Quantum Algorithms:

3.1 Quantum Support Vector Machines (QSVMs)

Quantum Support Vector Machines leverage quantum kernels to process data in very high-dimensional spaces (Schuld & Petruccione). The idea is that quantum circuits can compute kernel functions faster or more efficiently than classical SVMs. This concept is often discussed in quantum machine learning literature, especially in work highlighting early quantum advantages.

3.2 Variational Quantum Algorithms (VQAs)

Variational Quantum Algorithms are hybrid methods combining quantum circuits with classical optimisation. Parameterised quantum circuits (also called PQCs) are adjusted by a classical optimiser to minimise a cost function . Well-known examples include the Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) and the Quantum Approximate Optimisation Algorithm (QAOA), both used extensively on NISQ devices (Biamonte et al., 2017).

3.3 Quantum Reinforcement Learning (QRL)

Quantum reinforcement learning extends classical reinforcement learning by leveraging quantum effects, such as superposition, to accelerate exploration. While still mostly theoretical, QRL is discussed within broader quantum machine learning frameworks (Biamonte et al., 2017). This highlights an important distinction between algorithmic potential and real-world feasibility, as many quantum algorithms assume fault-tolerant hardware that is not yet available.

4. Challenges in Quantum Computing:

Quantum computing faces a number of serious challenges:

  • Noise and decoherence: Qubits lose information extremely easily due to environmental interactions (Nielsen & Chuang).
  • Error correction: Quantum error correction requires many physical qubits for one logical qubit, making it difficult with today’s hardware. As a result, current quantum computers should be viewed as experimental and complementary to classical systems rather than direct replacements.
  • Training PQCs: Variational circuits can suffer from barren plateaus and noise-related training difficulties (Benedetti et al., 2019). Algorithmic limits: Not all quantum algorithms outperform classical ones yet, especially on NISQ devices.

These issues make it difficult to build large-scale quantum systems.

5. Applications of Quantum Computing:

5.1 Space Exploration

Quantum computing may eventually help optimise spacecraft trajectories, analyse astronomical data, and design materials for extreme environments. Some of these ideas follow from the broader potential of quantum machine learning and quantum optimisation (Biamonte et al., 2017).

5.2 Cybersecurity and Pattern Recognition

Quantum machine learning models could help detect threats or unusual patterns much faster, building on ideas from QSVMs and quantum kernels (Schuld & Petruccione). At the same time, advances in quantum computing also motivate quantum-safe encryption.

5.3 Healthcare and Drug Discovery

Quantum computers could simulate molecules more accurately, speeding up drug discovery. Variational algorithms like VQE have already been proposed for simulating molecular energy levels (Benedetti et al., 2019), and supervised quantum learning methods may assist with pattern recognition in medical data .

Conclusion

Quantum computing represents a fundamental shift in computation, extending beyond classical binary logic through principles such as superposition, entanglement, and quantum interference. As discussed in this paper, these properties enable quantum systems to address certain classes of problems, particularly in cryptography, optimisation, and simulation, more efficiently than classical computers. However, despite these theoretical advantages, quantum computing remains constrained by significant practical limitations. Current Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) devices are highly susceptible to decoherence, gate errors, and limited scalability, restricting the real-world implementation of many quantum algorithms. Consequently, quantum computers should be viewed as complementary to classical systems rather than direct replacements. Looking ahead, progress in quantum error correction, hardware stability, and algorithm design will be crucial to the long-term viability of quantum computing. While large-scale, fault-tolerant systems remain a long-term objective, ongoing research indicates steady progress toward overcoming existing challenges. Ultimately, the impact of quantum computing will depend on successfully translating theoretical potential into practical, scalable technologies.
